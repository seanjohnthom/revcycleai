Four health systems just deployed AI to validate vendor invoices before payment. The technology is preventing $32 billion in overpayments annually across a $323 billion spend category.

But here's the twist: this isn't revenue cycle AI. It's supply chain AI.

And it's teaching us exactly what's broken about how we use AI in claims management.

What Happened

OSF HealthCare, Kettering Health, MemorialCare, and MUSC Health recently deployed SpendRule's AI platform to automate invoice validation for purchased services â€” everything from janitorial contracts to IT support to medical equipment maintenance.

The problem they were solving: Most invoices get approved without validation.

Health systems receive thousands of vendor invoices every month. Finance teams are supposed to verify each line item against the contract terms. But contracts are hundreds of pages long, sitting in filing cabinets (literal or digital), disconnected from the payment approval workflow.

So what happens? Invoices get rubber-stamped. And vendors overbill. A lot.

SpendRule's solution: Turn contract terms into code.

The AI encodes every pricing term, service level agreement, and payment condition from vendor contracts. Then it validates every invoice line automatically against those encoded terms before payment. Discrepancies get flagged instantly with evidence. No manual approvals needed.

The result? Health systems are catching overbilling before it happens, not chasing refunds after.

The $32 Billion Question

If AI can prevent $32 billion in vendor overpayments, why are we still reacting to claim denials after they hit?

Think about the parallels:

Vendor Invoices (Supply Chain)Claim Submissions (Revenue Cycle)

See the problem?

Revenue cycle teams are using AI reactively. We predict denials after the claim is already wrong. We automate appeals after we've already lost. We track A/R after cash flow is already damaged.

But supply chain teams are using AI proactively. They validate before payment. They catch errors upstream. They prevent problems instead of fixing them.

What Proactive Claims AI Looks Like

1. Encode Payor Policies Into Code

Just like SpendRule encodes vendor contracts, encode medical necessity criteria, prior auth requirements, and coverage rules. Make them machine-readable.

2. Validate Claims Before Submission

Run every claim through real-time policy validation. Flag coding errors, missing documentation, and eligibility mismatches before the claim goes out.

3. Automate Prior Auth Validation (Not Prediction)

Don't predict whether a service needs prior auth. Know it does. Encode the rules. Trigger auth workflows automatically when criteria are met.

What To Do Now (48-Hour Action Plan)

Step 1: Audit Your Top 10 Denial Reasons

Pull your denial data from the last 90 days. Identify the top 10 reasons. If more than 30% are "medical necessity" or "prior auth missing," you have a validation problem, not an appeal problem.

Step 2: Identify Which Denials Were Preventable

How many denials could have been caught before submission if someone had checked the payor policy? That's your opportunity cost.

Step 3: Encode One Payor's Medical Necessity Rules

Pick your highest-volume payor. Extract their medical necessity criteria for your top 5 CPT codes. Turn those criteria into a checklist. Validate claims against it before submission for 30 days. Measure denial rate change.

The Bigger Lesson

SpendRule's CTO said it best: "Most contracts sit in a filing cabinet, disconnected from the payments they're supposed to govern."

Swap "contracts" for "payor policies" and "payments" for "claims," and you've described revenue cycle management in 2026.

The breakthrough isn't the AI. It's the shift from reactive to proactive. From damage control to damage prevention. From predicting failures to preventing them.

Your claims are just invoices to payors. It's time to treat them like supply chain does.
